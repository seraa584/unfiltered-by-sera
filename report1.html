The Silent Epidemic: A Neuropsychological Look at Coerced Marriages

Published: October 25, 2025

The Moment: Contextualizing Coercion

A snapshot of the kind of hidden family dynamics or emotional coercion that often precedes and defines the complex human rights issue explored in this report.

1. The Context: A Global Overview

Forced marriage is defined by the UN as a union where one or both parties are married without their full and free consent. Globally, an estimated 22 million people are currently trapped in forced marriages (as per the ILO 2021 report). Our analysis begins by looking at the core mechanism of coercion: the systematic dismantling of individual autonomy, often starting long before the marriage ceremony itself.

2. The Neuropsychological Lens: Trauma and Coercion

The core of coercion is the systematic erosion of autonomy, and its effects are imprinted on the brain. We see victims exhibit traits linked to chronic, inescapable stress, often resulting in learned helplessness. This state is neurologically tied to the prefrontal cortex—the area responsible for executive function and conscious decision-making. When a person is constantly subjected to threats and control, the PFC's ability to plan escape or resist is diminished, making compliance the default survival mechanism. Simultaneously, the amygdala, the brain's alarm center, is kept in a state of high alert, driving perpetual hyper-vigilance and chronic anxiety that persist long after the immediate danger has passed. Understanding these neural correlates moves the conversation beyond moral judgment and toward effective, trauma-informed intervention.

3. Policy & Intervention: A Call to Action

Effective interventions for survivors must be rooted in neurobiology, aiming to reverse the effects of learned helplessness and chronic stress. This means moving beyond legal enforcement to prioritize specialized psychological care. We suggest three actionable pathways:

  • **Rebuilding Autonomy (PFC Focus):** Therapy and support programs must focus on small, consistent exercises that allow survivors to **regain decision-making control**. This helps rewire the prefrontal cortex, showing the brain that their choices matter.
  • **Somatic Regulation (Amygdala Focus):** Techniques like mindfulness and body-centered therapies are crucial for down-regulating the constantly activated amygdala, helping to reduce the persistent **fight-or-flight response** and anxiety.
  • **Community and Connection:** Social support networks combat the deep-seated isolation coercion creates. Restoring trust and secure attachment is essential for overall psychological recovery and neuroplastic healing.

Takeaway

The stories of those in hidden lives are not just human rights crises; they are powerful examples of the human brain's resilience and vulnerability under duress. By applying a neuroscientific lens, we can craft more effective, empathetic, and sustainable solutions.
